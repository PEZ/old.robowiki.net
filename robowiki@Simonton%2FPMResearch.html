    • The reason I don't think (intuitively) that multiple choice will work well against surfers is because surfers constantly change their movement, whereas multiple choice uses lots of data, possibly from a very long time ago.     • Well, I think your intuition is good ;) - you need to discard or decay data against surfers to get best results. It's hard for me to comment on the above results without really knowing the code, too, as there are lots of ways to do MultipleChoice. -- Voidious
  • Here's my reasoning why it will work better without matching on angular data: What I'm assuming is that the patterns a WaveSurfer generates when seeking the minimum danger will be patterns in velocity, and not angular velocity. The fact that my micro, symbolic, unfolded pattern matcher (in Decado) is able to enable the flattener in Dookious is testament to that. What I've done in Decado is actually removed the matching on angular velocity completely, instead assuming that the enemy will use whatever angular pattern they did last time their velocity matched this pattern. Why this works better (I think) is because potential matches don't get thrown out because the enemy happened to do a funny left/right angular jiggle, so we learn their pattern quicker. I plan to release a MiniBot soon which has Decado's gun, yet is folded. It may become a major ProblemBot for surfers (though not for my flattener idea in GoTo? surfing). -- Skilgannon

  • I've done it a couple ways. The "first stab" above includes a "wall segment history", which is some function of the distance from the enemy straight ahead to the wall (the value most bots use for wall smoothing). Then it just includes it as part of determining a match. The 1 value you see for "200 wall distance segmenting" calls 200 or closer a different segment, then uses it only to match the very first symbol (like more traditional segmenting). --Simonton

  • (Edit Conflict) I don't know if this is what you meant anyway, but "distance straight ahead to the wall" isn't quite what most top guns use for wall distance. The common method I know of is you project the enemy continuing to move in an orbit and come away with the GuessFactor they will be at when they hit the wall. So a GF of 1 or higher means they could orbit normally and not hit the wall before your bullet gets there, while something like 0.25 means they will definitely have to take the wall into consideration (WallSmoothing or bounce the other way) for this shot. -- Voidious
